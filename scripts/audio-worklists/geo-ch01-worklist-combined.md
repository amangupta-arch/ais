A community might refuse to mine a hill it holds sacred, or treat certain land or animals as not-for-use. Culture decides what we allow ourselves to use.

**Lesson 2: four-ways-to-classify-a-resource**

- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t01-k0-en.mp3`  _(tutor_message)_
  > Here's a question that sounds simple and isn't. Take a single forest. What kind of resource is it? Watch what happens when you try to answer, you'll find it's not one kind. It's four at once, and that's not a trick. It's exactly how geographers think.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t02-k0-en.mp3`  _(tutor_message)_
  > Geographers don't keep one big box labelled "resources". They keep four sorting questions, four lenses, and they run every resource through all four. Think of it like a suitcase with four luggage tags. The same suitcase gets a tag for where it came from, one for how it's owned, and so on. One object, four tags.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t03-k0-en.mp3`  _(svg_graphic)_
  > Every resource gets sorted four ways at once: origin, exhaustibility, ownership, and stage of development.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t04-k0-en.mp3`  _(tutor_message)_
  > Lens 1, Origin. Where did it come from, the living world or the non-living world? - Biotic resources come from the biosphere and have life: forests, fish, livestock, people themselves. - Abiotic resources are made of non-living things: rocks, minerals, metals. A forest? Biotic. First tag on the suitcase.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t05-k0-en.mp3`  _(tutor_message)_
  > Lens 2, Exhaustibility. Will it run out? - Renewable resources renew themselves, solar, wind, water, forests, wildlife. Some are continuous (the sun won't notice us); some need time to come back (a chopped forest, a drained aquifer). - Non-renewable resources took millions of years to form, minerals, fossil fuels. Use them up and they're gone on any human timescale. A forest renews itself if we let it. Renewable. Second tag.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t06-k0-en.mp3`  _(media)_
  > Renewable on the left, non-renewable on the right, the difference is time.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t07-k0-en.mp3`  _(tutor_message)_
  > Lens 3, Ownership. Who does it belong to? - Individual, a private well, a family's farmland. - Community, the village grazing common, the public pond. - National, everything within a country's borders: its minerals, forests, rivers, and the sea up to 12 nautical miles from shore. - International, the open ocean beyond 200 km from any coast. No single country owns it; global institutions govern it. Most of India's forests are managed by the nation. National. Third tag.
- file: `geo-ch01-c1l2-t08-k0-en.mp3`  _(tutor_message)_
  > Lens 4, Status of development. This is the one students mix up, so slow down here. - Potential, it exists in a region but we haven't tapped it yet (Rajasthan's solar and wind). - Developed, surveyed, measured, and actively in use. - Stock, the material is right there, but we don't yet have the technology to use it. Water is hydrogen and oxygen, an enormous fuel, but we can't unlock it at scale. So it's stock. - Reserve, the slice of stock we can use with today's technology but haven't started, like a standing forest kept for the future. The forest we're saving for later? Reserve. Fourth tag.
