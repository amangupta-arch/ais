## The choice

`/math-quiz-test` runs every selected image through `compressImage(dataUrl, maxDim=1280, quality=0.85)` before posting to `/api/ai/math-quiz`. Implementation uses `<canvas>` — no new dependency.

## The bug we were fixing

A learner uploaded a 5 MB photo. Browser fetch POSTed it as base64-in-JSON to our route. Vercel's serverless function rejected the request body with a 413 → returned `"Request Entity Too Large"` as plain text. Our client did `await res.json()` on it and threw:

> `SyntaxError: Unexpected token 'R', "Request En"... is not valid JSON`

## Why client-side

- **Vercel body limit (4.5 MB)** is the hard ceiling. Anything we do server-side has already crossed it.
- **Less Anthropic vision tokens** — fewer pixels through the model = lower cost and lower latency. 1280 px is plenty for handwriting recognition.
- **No new dep** — `<canvas>.toDataURL("image/jpeg", 0.85)` ships with the browser.

## What 1280 px / 0.85 quality gets us

- Typical 12 MP phone photo: 3–6 MB → ~200–400 KB.
- Quality is plenty for handwriting; you can still read every digit.
- Anthropic vision is happy.

## Where else this could apply

Any future feature that uploads camera images (paper notes, whiteboard captures, photo-of-screen feedback) should reuse `compressImage()`. Hoist it into `lib/util/` when there's a second caller.

## Trade-offs

- We lose the original-resolution image. For paper grading we don't need it, but for any feature that needs forensic detail (scanning a multi-step proof in tiny handwriting), we'd want a higher `maxDim`.
- Canvas compression is single-threaded and synchronous-ish; on a low-end Android, ~200 ms for a 5 MB photo. Tolerable.
